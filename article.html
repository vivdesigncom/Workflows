Where does the responsibility fall when training management programs fall down? Part 1

Friday, November 29, 2013 6:00:01 AM

I hear and read more and more these days about the challenges organizations face to remain competitive in their industry or market place. Customer satisfaction will ultimately determine which companies are actually busy and which ones are only busy trying to find customers. There are many factors that determine the level of customer satisfaction. These may include cost, quality, on time delivery and many others. Without a formal survey system it can be difficult to measure customer satisfaction. In many cases even surveys don’t communicate true satisfaction. When was the last time you actually took the time to complete a satisfaction survey? Let me ask a better question. When was the last time you felt the need to complain about a product or service that you purchased? Sometimes it may be easier to measure customer “dissatisfaction” because customers might not always complete a survey, e-mailor call to inform you that everything is fine when they are happy with you, but you can be sure they will let you know when they are not. So what does all of this have to do with training and human resource management programs? To answer that, I would like to look at one of those factors that lead to customer satisfaction or dissatisfaction depending on which one you measure. That factor is quality. For the purpose of this article I will focus on “product quality” please feel free to interpret this as quality of service if this is more applicable to your industry.

I’m not going to go down that road of design/process FMEAs and APQP etc. and the roles these play with product quality, let’s assume these are complete and were done well. The responsibility for quality of the final product is now in the hands of the production operator or service provider etc. Here is where the rubber meets the road and where training or better yet “effective training” takes center stage.The correlation between product quality and effective training cannot be denied. If you don’t believe this, consider these statistics. The National Safety Council compiled an odds-of-dying table for 2008, which illustrates the relative risks of flying versus driving safety. It calculated the odds of dying in a motor vehicle accident to be 1 in 98 for a lifetime. For air and space transport (including air taxis and private flights), the odds were 1 in 7,178 for a lifetime. Now considering this fact, over 90% of car accidents are caused by driver error vs. the 50% of plane crashes that are cause by pilot error, one could agree that perhaps pilot training is more effective than driver training. So when we ask the question; where does the responsibility fall when training management programs or training processes fall down? Fingers usually point to the Human Resources department or manager. We are notexcusingthis group from recruiting, hiring and new employee orientation but what do these things have to do with specific job requirements that impact quality, training definition and the effectiveness of this training.

Who should we audit when assessing conformance of training processes and when auditing exposes nonconformance’s, who should the NCR/CAR be assigned to?

Stay tuned for Part 2 of this discussion.

Ragnar Schreier is the author of this article on Training Management Programs and Human Resource Management Programs.
